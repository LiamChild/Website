Past Train Fleet

Class: C-set

Info: The C sets were a class of electric multiple units that operated on Sydney's suburban rail network from 1986 up until 2021. Built by A Goninan & Co between 1986 and 1987, they were introduced into service by the State Rail Authority, before later being operated under CityRail and Sydney Trains. A total of 56 carriages were built, with the last sets being withdrawn from service in February 2021, having been gradually replaced by A & B set trains.

Class: S-set

Info: The S sets are a class of electric multiple units that operated on Sydney's suburban rail network from 1972 up until 2019. Originally entering service under the Public Transport Commission, the sets also operated under the State Rail Authority, CityRail and Sydney Trains. Prior to their retirement, the S sets were the last class in the Sydney Trains fleet to not be air-conditioned, earning them the nicknames "Tin cans" and "Sweat Sets". They were also nicknamed "Ridgys" because of their fluted ("ridged") stainless steel panelling; they shared this nickname with similar looking K sets and C sets. Their stainless steel appearance was also shared with the intercity V sets and U sets. All remaining sets were withdrawn from service in June 2019.

Class: U-set

Info: The U sets were a type of electric multiple unit (EMU) operated by the New South Wales Government Railways and its successors between September 1958 and November 1996. They ran on the Intercity network. They were nicknamed U-boats.

Class: Bradfield Stock

Info: The New South Wales Bradfield suburban carriage stock, also known as the "Bradfield Cars," were the first electric train suburban carriages used by the New South Wales Government Railways (NSWGR) in Sydney, Australia. These carriages were introduced in the late 1920s and early 1930s as part of the electrification of the Sydney suburban rail network.

Named after Dr. John Bradfield, the chief engineer behind the Sydney Harbour Bridge and the city's electrification project, these carriages were designed to be robust and suitable for high-frequency urban service. The Bradfield Cars featured steel construction and were initially fitted with timber interiors. They were recognizable by their distinctive "dog box" design, with separate compartments and no central corridor.

The Bradfield suburban carriage stock remained in service until the 1970s, when they were gradually phased out and replaced by more modern rolling stock. Some of these carriages have been preserved by railway heritage organizations and are occasionally displayed at transport museums or during special heritage events.

Class: Standard Stock

Info: The New South Wales Standard suburban carriage stock are a class of electric multiple units that were operated by the New South Wales Government Railways and its successors between 1926 and 1992. They served on the Sydney suburban network. In the years before their withdrawal, they were nicknamed Red Rattlers.

Class: Sputnik Stock

Info: The New South Wales Sputnik suburban carriage stock is a type of electric multiple unit that was operated by the New South Wales Government Railways and its successors between 1957 and 1993 and served on the Sydney rail network. These trains, as well as other types of single-deck suburban electrics that were first manufactured in the 1920s, are known by Sydneysiders as "Red Rattlers".

Class: Tulloch Stock

Info: The New South Wales Tulloch suburban carriage stock were a type of electric multiple unit in a single deck - double deck - double deck - single deck unit. Operated by the New South Wales Government Railways and its successors between 1940 and 1992. In their later years, they were nicknamed Red Rattlers.

Class: 620 and 720 Railcars

Info: The 620 and 720 rail cars are Australian suburban electric multiple units (EMUs) used primarily by the New South Wales Government Railways (now part of Sydney Trains). The 620 class, built between 1961 and 1968, were designed for short- to medium-distance travel, featuring a diesel-mechanical design with two-car sets comprising a driving motor and trailer car. The 720 class is essentially the same as the 620 class, with differences mainly in the interiors and intended uses. Both classes served in various regional and suburban services, particularly on the Hunter and Illawarra lines, and were known for their reliability and simplicity, contributing to their long service life, with some units remaining operational into the early 2000s.

Class: 660 and 760 Railcars

Info: The NSW 660 and 760 rail cars were a series of diesel multiple units (DMUs) operated by the New South Wales Government Railways in Australia. Introduced between 1961 and 1965, these rail cars were designed to provide regional and rural passenger services across the state. The 660 class rail cars featured two-engine configurations, while the 760 class had a similar design but with different seating arrangements and internal fittings to accommodate various service requirements. Both classes were known for their reliability and were used extensively on branch lines until they were gradually phased out in the late 1980s and early 1990s due to modernization efforts and the introduction of newer rolling stock.

Class: Tulloch Trailers

Info:The NSW Tulloch Trailers, also known as the Tulloch Rail Trailers, were a series of passenger carriages built by Tulloch Limited for the New South Wales Government Railways (NSWGR) in Australia during the 1950s and 1960s. These carriages were distinctive for their lightweight aluminum construction, which made them more fuel-efficient and reduced track wear compared to their steel counterparts. They were primarily used on suburban and interurban routes, providing increased comfort and reliability to passengers with their improved seating and amenities. Despite their eventual replacement by newer rolling stock, the Tulloch Trailers played a significant role in modernizing the NSWGR fleet and enhancing the overall commuter experience in New South Wales during their period of service.

Class: Silver City Comet

Info: The NSW Silver City Comet was a distinctive rail service in New South Wales, Australia, that operated from 1937 to 1989. Named after the "Silver City," Broken Hill, the train was the first fully air-conditioned diesel-powered train in the Southern Hemisphere and was recognized for its distinctive silver color. It provided a vital link between Parkes, in central New South Wales, and Broken Hill, running through remote and sparsely populated areas. Renowned for its comfort and reliability, the Silver City Comet was a significant advancement in Australian railway history, offering passengers a faster and more comfortable journey compared to the steam locomotives of its time. The service was eventually discontinued in 1989 due to declining passenger numbers and the rising costs of operation, but it remains a celebrated part of Australian rail heritage.
